How to play Chess




Chess


Chess is a turn based strategy board game played between 2 players on an 8x8 chequered board. Each player picks a side either black or white and the main objective is to trap your opponents king which is called a Checkmate, the player can either win through a checkmate, draw or by resigning.

The player with the white pieces goes first and every subsequent turn the players will take turns to play a move. Each turn, the player has to move 1 piece to a new square. There are 6 unique pieces in Chess and each piece has their own unique moveset and properties which we will go into detail later. To capture an opponent piece, simply move your piece onto the square occupied by your opponent and remove it from the board granted it is a legal move.


The board


The board must always be placed so that each player has a light coloured square in their bottom right hand corner. Placeing the board the wrong way round is a very common mistake. Once it is the right way round, we can start adding the pieces. Each player's pieces start on the first rank on that player's side of the board.(horizontal rows are called ranks)


Ranks are labelled below as letters, the top being A all the way down to H, and coloumns have their numbers 1 to 8. This way each square on their board has their own id making it easier to represent moves in text.



Recording a move


Each pieve is represented by the capitalised first letter of its name except for the Knight as it starts with the same letter as the king so it uses the letter N instead. When recording a move, we take the pieve that is being moved and the square that it is moving to.

Example:


  • Kf1 - King moves to f1 square
  • Nf3 - Knight moves to f3 square
  • Pe4 - Pawn moves to e4

When a piece captures another piece it is repsented as an 'x' symbol between the piece and the square. So it would be Nxf3.


When a pawn reaches the end of the board an promotes it is represented as an '=' symbol between the piece and the square.


For check and checkmate a '+' and '#' are used respectively after the square so Ba3+ or Qxh7#.

Piece introduction: Pawns




The pawn is seemingly the weakest of the bunch. However, its role in Chess is not to be underestimated. Each player starts the game with eight pawns on the second rank.



Pawns: Movement


Except on its first move, the pawn is only allowed to move one square forwards, and never backwards. On its very first move, a pawn has the choice of moving one or two squares, although both squares must be unoccupied to do this. On all subsequent moves, the pawn can move one square only, even if it only moved one square on its first turn - it only has one chance to move two.



Pawns: Capturing


If the square directly in front of a pawn is occupied, the pawn is blocked and cannot move forwards. Unlike all the other pieces, a pawn does not capture the same way it moves. Instead, a pawn captures by moving one square diagonally forward. In the diagram below, the white pawn cannot capture the black pawn directly in front of it, but it may capture the black knight by moving one square diagonally forward. Try it for yourself - drag the white pawn onto the black knight to capture it!



Pawns: Promotion


The most important feature of pawns is their ability to promote when they reach the opposite side of the board. If your pawn gets all the way to the opponent's back rank, it is immediately promoted to a more powerful piece - take the pawn off the board, and replace it with a new piece of your choice. The only restriction is that you cannot promote to a king or another pawn. Usually, a pawn is promoted to a queen, as the queen is the most powerful piece, but sometimes there is a good reason to promote to a different piece such as a knight. It doesn't matter if you have all your pieces still on the board, you can still promote to whatever piece you want, even if this means you have two or three queens on the board at once.

Piece introduction: Bishops




The Bishop is a long range piece that can be very powerful, but it has a significant weakness - it can only ever move on squares of one colour. Each player starts the game with two bishops - one that moves on light squares, and one that moves on dark squares - so between them, the two bishops can cover all the squares on the board.



Bishops: Movement


The bishop can move any number of squares in a straight diagonal line, so long as the squares between the bishop and its destination are unoccupied. The diagram below shows the legal moves for a bishop.

Piece introduction: Rooks




The rooks, which are also sometimes known as 'castles', are the second most powerful piece after the queen. Like the bishop they are long range pieces, but they do not share the bishop's weakness and can reach every square on the board. Because they start in the corners, rooks often don't join in the battle until after the knights and bishops, but that also means they often survive much deeper into the game. In fact, the most commonly occurring endgames in chess are endgames involving rooks! Each player starts the game with two rooks.



Rooks: Movement


The rook can move any number of squares in a straight line horizontally or vertically, so long as none of the squares it passes through are occupied. The board below shows the legal moves for a rook:

Piece introduction: Queens




The mighty queen is the most powerful piece on the chessboard, combining the powers of the rook and bishop. Such is the queen's power, that the player who loses his or her queen is almost sure to lose the game as well. No other piece rivals the speed and manoeuverability of the queen, and many games are often decided by a lightning queen sortie. Each player starts the game with one queen.



Queens: Movement


The queen can move any number of squares in a straight line horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. In this way, she combines the powers of the rook and bishop in a single piece. The legal moves of the queen are displayed on the board below:

Piece introduction: Kings




The king is the most important piece in the game. You must keep your king safe at all costs, because if your opponent traps your king, it's checkmate and you lose the game. Don't worry though, the king is not completely helpless and he has an army of loyal pieces and pawns to protect him! Each player starts the game with one king, and unlike the other pieces and pawns, the king is never captured and stays on the board for the whole game.



Kings: Movement
